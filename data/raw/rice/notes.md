## 1. Rice import tariff rates and levies (composition + 2023-2025 changes)

- **Standard (non-integrated) rice import duty: 10% duty + 60% levy = 70% combined effective tariff** on foreign rice imports arriving by sea/authorized channels. — USDA FAS, *Nigeria: Grain and Feed Annual* (Report NI2024-0002), March 13, 2024. https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Grain+and+Feed+Annual_Lagos_Nigeria_NI2024-0002.pdf
- **Alternate/"backward integration" rate for millers**: an integrated rice miller with a confirmed backward integration program (i.e., proof it also sources/grows domestic paddy) is eligible for a reduced **10% import duty + 20% levy = 30% combined** rate to import rice — same USDA FAS report as above, March 13, 2024.
- **Rough (paddy) rice import duty: 20%** — same USDA FAS report, March 13, 2024.
- U.S. Government Trade.gov Country Commercial Guide (last updated **September 5, 2025**) independently confirms: *"In agriculture, wheat (85%), sugar (75%), rice (70%), and tomato paste (50%) see the highest supplemental tariffs."* — https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/nigeria-import-tariffs (accessed 2026-07-07). This corroborates the ~70% effective rate on ordinary imports as still current as of mid-2025.
- VAT: husked brown rice and other basic cereals (maize, rice, wheat, millet, barley, sorghum, oats, fonio, finer millet — raw or semi-processed) are **VAT-exempt** under the VAT Act (Modification) Order 2021. — KPMG Nigeria Tax Newsletter, Issue No. 8.2, **August 2024**. (PDF: "The Nigeria Customs Service Issues Guidelines for the Implementation of Zero Duty Rate on Some Basic Food Items")
- Historical context: Nigeria's rice tariff was already **70%** as far back as 2013, well above the ECOWAS CET ceiling (bound rates for Nigeria's "sensitive"/List-D-type products have at times reportedly reached ~110-120% combined duty+levy in various sources) — World Bank Africa Can blog, "Nigeria's border closure: a road block or a speed bump on the road to a successful AfCFTA?" https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/africacan/nigerias-border-closure-road-block-or-speed-bump-road-successful-afcfta (accessed 2026-07-07). **Gap/caution**: I could not independently verify an exact "110%" or "120%" combined figure from a single authoritative primary source (e.g., Nigeria Customs Service CET schedule or WTO tariff profile) within this session — treat any >70% historical figure as reported-but-not-triple-confirmed.
- **2023 tariff increase**: The Federal Government raised the tariff on rice imports "in packing of more than 5kg or in bulk and in packing of 5kg or less" **to 60% from 50%** — Businessday NG, "FG raises tariff for rice to 60%, wheat 70%," https://businessday.ng/news/article/fg-raises-tariff-for-rice-to-60-wheat-70/ (accessed 2026-07-07; exact article date not independently confirmed in this session — flagged as a gap, should be re-verified for precise 2023 date before citing in final work).
- **January 2024**: Central Bank of Nigeria raised customs duty benchmark exchange rate calculation basis, effectively raising customs duties by **2.6%** (import duties are benchmarked against the naira/dollar rate) — cited in USDA FAS Grain and Feed Annual, March 13, 2024 (citing a news source; original news source not independently re-verified).
- **October 2023**: CBN lifted foreign exchange restrictions for the importation of **43 items, including rice and corn** — USDA FAS Grain and Feed Annual, March 13, 2024.

## 2. The 2019-2020 land border closure: legacy and current (2023-2025) smuggling status

- **Timeline**: Partial closure with Benin announced **August 2019**; expanded to **all land borders** with Benin, Cameroon, Chad, and Niger on **October 14, 2019** ("total control over what comes in" — Nigeria Customs Comptroller-General, quoted). — Council on Foreign Relations, "Buhari Orders Land Borders Closed in Long-Running Effort to Boost Rice Production," published **October 17, 2019**. https://www.cfr.org/articles/buhari-orders-land-borders-closed-long-running-effort-boost-rice-production
- Precursor: Nigeria had already restricted FX access for rice importers in **June 2015**, which is credited with driving up Benin-route smuggling in the years before the 2019 closure — same CFR source.
- **Reopening**: Nigeria announced a **partial reopening of four land borders on December 16, 2020** (effective from **December 2020 / early 2021**), initially for light vehicles/pedestrians, later goods trade — Africanews, "Nigeria to reopen border with Benin for goods trade," Dec 17, 2020, https://www.africanews.com/2020/12/17/nigeria-to-reopen-border-with-benin-for-goods-trade/; and allAfrica.com, "Nigeria: Rice, Poultry, Others Remained Banned As Govt Reopens 4 Borders," Dec 2020, https://allafrica.com/stories/202012170079.html.
- Crucially, **rice (and poultry) remained on the import-prohibition list even after the December 2020 reopening** — allAfrica.com, Dec 2020 (above). Africa Check separately confirms the Seme/Benin border **physically reopened in 2020, not 2023** as some social media claims suggested — Africa Check, "Nigeria's vital Seme land border with Benin reopened in 2020 not 2023 as claimed online," https://africacheck.org/fact-checks/meta-programme-fact-checks/nigerias-vital-seme-land-border-benin-reopened-2020-not-2023 (accessed 2026-07-07; full text of this article could not be fetched directly — 403 error — relying on the search-result summary snippet, so treat with some caution and re-verify original wording if quoting).
- **Effectiveness question**: Multiple sources agree smuggling **did not stop** after the closure — "the phenomenon hasn't stopped since the closure, raising questions about the measure's effectiveness" — ISS Africa, "Nigeria's border closures haven't served their purpose," https://issafrica.org/iss-today/nigerias-border-closures-havent-served-their-purpose (accessed 2026-07-07).
- **2023-2025 status: smuggling remains a major, active, and apparently worsening issue.** Concrete 2025 evidence:
  - **Q1 2025**: Nigeria Customs Service recorded **159 rice-related seizure cases, totaling 135,474 bags valued at ₦939 million** — rice was the **single most-smuggled commodity** in that quarter, ahead of petroleum products (61 cases, ₦43m) and narcotics (22 cases, ₦730m). Comptroller-General Adewale Adeniyi: seizures "reflect the persistent challenges we face in curbing smuggling." — PRNigeria, "Rice Smuggling Tops Customs' Seizure List in Q1 - CG Adeniyi," published **April 24, 2025**. https://prnigeria.com/2025/04/24/rice-smuggling-tops-customs/
  - **July 29–August 2, 2025**: Coordinated patrols along Tongeji Creek, Moba Waterside, and Badagry-Porto Novo Creek seized **823 bags of rice** concealed in boats destined for Badagry and neighboring communities — Sahara Reporters, "How Nigerian Customs Operatives Aid Rice Smuggling Into Lagos, Collect N250,000 Per Boat," published **July 30, 2025**. https://saharareporters.com/2025/07/30/how-nigerian-customs-operatives-aid-rice-smuggling-lagos-collect-n250000-boat
  - **Sept 1–Oct 9, 2025**: Customs Seme Area Command seized contraband (including rice) worth **₦1.99 billion** along the Lagos–Abidjan corridor — Information Nigeria, "Customs Seme Command Seizes N1.99bn Worth Of Contraband Along Lagos–Abidjan Corridor," published **October 2025**. https://www.informationng.com/2025/10/customs-seme-command-seizes-n1-99bn-worth-of-contraband-along-lagos-abidjan-corridor.html
  - Allegation (unverified/allegation-level, treat as a claim not a confirmed fact): a network including some Army, Police, and Customs personnel allegedly collects **₦4–6 billion weekly** facilitating smuggling (rice, contraband, vehicles) through the Seme–Badagry axis and Idi Iroko routes — Sahara Reporters, "Nigerian Customs, Police, Others Escort Smuggled Goods, Pocket N4–6Billion Weekly Through Cartels At Seme–Badagry Border –Sources," published **November 23, 2025**. https://saharareporters.com/2025/11/23/nigerian-customs-police-others-escort-smuggled-goods-pocket-n4-6billion-weekly-through
  - USDA FAS (March 2024) also independently notes the naira's steep 2023 devaluation vs. the West Africa CFA franc (naira depreciated ~72% against the CFA franc over the 12 months to early 2024) has been altering — in some periods reducing, in others encouraging — informal cross-border grain flows, showing the smuggling dynamic is exchange-rate-sensitive, not just tariff-driven. — USDA FAS Grain and Feed Annual (NI2024-0002), March 13, 2024.
- **Bottom line**: The 2019-2020 closure was **only ever partially reversed** — general goods trade reopened from Dec 2020, but rice (and poultry) remained formally banned/restricted, and informal/smuggled rice flows from Benin persisted throughout and remain a live, actively-reported problem through at least November 2025.

## 3. 2023-2025 emergency import waivers / duty-free windows for rice

- **President Tinubu approved a 150-day duty-free import window** for select food commodities — **maize, husked brown rice, wheat, grain sorghum, millet, and beans** — running **from 15 July 2024 to 31 December 2024**. This was announced **July 8, 2024** by Senator Abubakar Kyari, Minister of Agriculture and Food Security, as part of the **Presidential Accelerated Stabilisation and Advancement Plan (PASAP)**. — The News (Nigeria), "President Tinubu Approves 150-Day Duty Free Import Window for Food Commodities," published **July 8, 2024**. https://thenewsnigeria.com.ng/2024/07/08/president-tinubu-approves-150-day-duty-free-import-window-for-food-commodities/
- **Official implementation guidance**: Nigeria Customs Service (NCS) released guidelines for a **"Zero percent (0%) duty rate and VAT exemption"** on the same six items, effective **15 July 2024 – 31 December 2024**, "in furtherance of government's policy to enhance food supply... and reduce the soaring prices of foodstuffs." — KPMG Nigeria, "The Nigeria Customs Service Issues Guidelines for the Implementation of Zero Duty Rate on Some Basic Food Items," Tax Newsletter Issue No. 8.2, **August 2024**.
  - **Eligibility conditions** (per KPMG summary of the NCS guidelines):
    - Importer must be a Nigerian company in business for **over 5 years**, with 5 years of filed annual returns/financials and tax/payroll compliance.
    - Importers of **husked brown rice, grain sorghum, or millet** must own a **milling plant with minimum 100 tonnes/day production capacity**, operating for **at least 4 years**, with **adequate farmland for cultivation** (i.e., backward-integration proof).
    - Importers of maize, wheat, or beans must be agricultural companies with sufficient developed farmland, or feed-mill/agro-processing companies with an out-grower network.
    - Must register with the **Federal Ministry of Finance (FMoF)** for approved status and quota confirmation; must keep detailed activity records subject to government audit.
    - **At least 75% of the imported item must be traded on a recognized commodity exchange.**
    - **Non-compliance penalty**: forfeiture of all waivers, and the company becomes liable for the full VAT, levies, and import duties otherwise due — including if it re-sells the imported item (raw or processed) to other countries.
  - Context cited by KPMG: **food inflation hit 39.53% by end of July 2024** per National Bureau of Statistics.
- **Context/background**: The policy responded to food inflation that exceeded **32%** by mid-2024 (rising further to 39.53% by end-July per NBS) and was explicitly tied to the wider "food security" push by the Tinubu administration. — AgroCentric, "Temporary Suspension of Import Duties on Essential Agricultural Commodities in Nigeria," published **April 10, 2025** (retrospective analysis); KPMG Aug 2024 (contemporaneous).
- **Implementation friction reported**: as of early August 2024, importers said Nigeria Customs had not yet begun applying the zero-duty policy on the ground and "status quo on import duties [was] maintained" — Sahara Reporters, "Tinubu's Directive On Food Importation Ignored By Nigerian Customs, Status Quo On Import Duties Maintained –Importers," published **August 5, 2024**. https://saharareporters.com/2024/08/05/tinubus-directive-food-importation-ignored-nigerian-customs-status-quo-import-duties (headline/date captured via search; full article not independently fetched in this session — flagged as secondary-sourced).
- **Expiry / no confirmed extension found**: The window was legally bounded to **31 December 2024**. My searches (including a retrospective April 2025 AgroCentric analysis) found **no confirmed extension of the zero-duty rice window into 2025**. Separately, by late 2024/2025, reporting indicates import licenses under some related scheme were being granted selectively — e.g., one November 2025 mill-industry report states "selective import licenses granted only to Imota Rice Mill and BUA Foods" — AgriInsite, "Nigeria: Local rice mills under pressure as imported rice gains market share," published **November 3, 2025**. https://agriinsite.com/nigeria-local-rice-mills-under-pressure-as-imported-rice-gains-market-share/. **This is a gap**: I could not confirm the specific legal/policy basis, dates, or full terms of this apparent 2025 "Imota + BUA only" import licensing arrangement from a primary/official source — flagging as unverified and worth follow-up research.
- Separate 2026 fiscal policy signal (unconfirmed in this session — fetch failed): a headline reference exists to Nigeria "slashing import duties on cars, rice, palm oil in 2026 fiscal policy" (Nigerian Communications Week) but I was unable to retrieve or verify the article content due to a connection error. **Flagged as an unverified lead**, not used as a cited fact.

## 4. Local rice production data (2023-2025)

- **USDA FAS official Nigeria rice Production, Supply & Distribution (PSD) table** (Report NI2024-0002, published March 13, 2024):
  | Marketing Year | Rough (paddy) production | Milled production | Area harvested | Yield (rough) |
  |---|---|---|---|---|
  | 2022/2023 | 8.5 MMT | 5.355 MMT | 3.5 million ha | 2.4286 MT/ha |
  | 2023/2024 (estimate) | 8.3 MMT | 5.229 MMT | 3.5 million ha | 2.3714 MT/ha |
  | 2024/2025 (FAS-Lagos forecast) | **7.7 MMT (a 7% decrease)** | 4.851 MMT | 3.25 million ha | 2.3692 MT/ha |
  - FAS-Lagos attributes the projected 2024/25 decline to **insecurity in northern rice-producing regions, decreased government support, and inadequate mechanization**, plus reduced planting area from high input-cost inflation.
  - Source: https://apps.fas.usda.gov/newgainapi/api/Report/DownloadReportByFileName?fileName=Grain+and+Feed+Annual_Lagos_Nigeria_NI2024-0002.pdf
- **Alternative/independent figures** (CARD/riceforafrica.net, "Nigeria, September 2025" country update, accessed 2026-07-07, https://riceforafrica.net/nigeria-september-2025/):

  - Area harvested: **4.39 million ha (2020) → 4.6 million ha (2024)**.
  - Yield: "more or less stagnant at ~2 ton/ha" over 2020-2024 (note: this is somewhat lower than the USDA FAS yield figures above — the two sources are not fully reconciled; **flagging as a data discrepancy** between USDA FAS and CARD/riceforafrica figures for the same years, likely due to differing methodology, i.e. rough vs. adjusted paddy tonnage).
  - **Self-sufficiency index: 66.4% (2021) → 68.95% (2024)**.
  - Domestic consumption estimated at **7.7 MMT**, leaving a deficit of roughly **3.6 MMT** met via imports (implied, per this source's framing).

- **Major producers / mills**:
  - **WACOT Rice** (Kebbi State, part of Tropical General Investments/TGI Group): initial capacity **120,000 MT/year** (a ₦10 billion investment), later expanded/reported to **~250,000 MT/year**. — AgriInsite, Nov 3, 2025 (capacity figures); https://wacotrice.com/about-us/ (company background, accessed 2026-07-07).
  - **Labana Rice Mills** (Kebbi State): began production November 2014 with two plants totaling **400 MT/day**; a third plant under construction targeted **300 MT/day** more, which would bring total capacity to **700 MT/day**, described in sourcing as potentially "Africa's largest rice factory" (claim as reported, not independently verified against a neutral source).
  - **Coscharis Farms/Rice** (Anambra State): cultivating **2,500 hectares** in "Phase 1," with a planned **5,000-hectare** out-grower scheme.

## 5. Import volumes/values for rice (2023-2025)

- **USDA FAS trade estimates** (Grain and Feed Annual, NI2024-0002, published March 13, 2024):
  - MY 2022/2023 imports: **2.275 MMT** (MY basis) / 2.0 MMT (TY basis)
  - MY 2023/2024 imports: **1.9 MMT** (MY basis) / 2.0 MMT (TY basis)
  - MY 2024/2025 (forecast): **1.5 MMT**, a projected **21% decrease** from MY2023/24, attributed to high global rice prices and naira devaluation reducing import demand relative to neighboring-country buyers.
  - Nigeria recorded **zero rice exports** in all periods shown (2022/23 through 2024/25 forecast) — i.e., Nigeria is a pure rice importer, no formal export volume.
- **Earlier USDA-based media projection** (pre-dating the March 2024 FAS Annual, so treat as an earlier/superseded estimate): Nigeria was projected to import **2.1 million metric tons of rice in 2024**, potentially making it the **top global rice buyer** that year, with an estimated import bill of **approximately US$1.31 billion** (2.3 MMT × ~US$568/ton). — Nairametrics, "Nigeria to import 2.1 million metric tons of rice, become top buyer globally in 2024 – USDA," published **November 15, 2023**. https://nairametrics.com/2023/11/15/nigeria-to-import-2-1-million-metric-tons-of-rice-become-top-buyer-globally-in-2024/. **Note**: this figure (2.1 MMT for calendar 2024) is somewhat higher than the later FAS-Lagos MY2024/25 estimate of 1.5 MMT above — the discrepancy likely reflects (a) calendar-year vs. marketing-year framing, and (b) the November 2023 estimate predates the sharper 2024 naira devaluation that suppressed import demand. **Flagging as a data discrepancy needing reconciliation** rather than picking one number as "the" 2024 import figure.
- **NBS Foreign Trade in Goods Statistics**: I located the NBS quarterly report series (Q3 2023, Q4 2023, Q1 2024, Q2 2024 — all at nigerianstat.gov.ng) which contains Nigeria's aggregate merchandise trade figures (e.g., total Q4 2023 trade of ₦26,801.95 billion; full-year 2023 imports of ₦35,917.62 billion), but **I was not able to extract a rice-specific commodity-level import value or volume line from these reports within this session** — the aggregate totals are macro (all-goods) figures, not disaggregated to the HS code for rice. **This is a clear gap**: obtaining the exact NBS rice-specific import value/volume for 2023-2025 would require opening the detailed commodity tables inside the NBS PDF/Excel releases (e.g., https://www.nigerianstat.gov.ng/pdfuploads/FTS_Q1_2024.pdf and similar quarterly files), which was not completed in this pass.

## 6. AfCFTA link — rice as a "sensitive"/excluded product

- Nigeria has placed **rice among products excluded/protected from AfCFTA tariff liberalization**, alongside **cement, flour (meal/powder), sugar, cocoyam flour, and approximately 180 other products** on its exclusion/sensitive list. — Guardian Nigeria, "Rice, flour, sugar, cement top free trade exemption list" (headline and figures captured via search-result summary; **direct fetch of the article returned a 403 error, so the full text/exact publication date could not be independently confirmed in this session** — flagged as secondary-sourced, worth re-verifying original Guardian article date before final citation). https://guardian.ng/business-services/rice-flour-sugar-cement-top-free-trade-exemption-list/
- Specifically: *"semi-milled or wholly milled rice, whether or not polished or glazed"* is named among the protected tariff lines, per the same source.
- **General AfCFTA tariff liberalization framework** (context, not Nigeria-specific): under the AfCFTA Protocol on Trade in Goods, **97% of tariff lines** are to be progressively liberalized over **10 years** (13 years for Least Developed Countries); **7% are categorized as "sensitive products"** with a longer transition; and up to **3%** may be **fully excluded** from liberalization for reasons of food security, national security, fiscal revenue, livelihood protection, or industrialization policy. Rice appears to fall within Nigeria's "excluded" (not just "sensitive/delayed") category per the Guardian reporting above. — General AfCFTA structure cited via search-result summary referencing tralac/macmap-type sources; **no single primary legal document (e.g., Nigeria's official AfCFTA tariff offer schedule) was directly opened in this session** to confirm the exact tariff-line count or legal citation. **This is a gap**: for a rigorous BCG/McKinsey-grade citation, the ideal next step is to pull Nigeria's actual AfCFTA Schedule of Tariff Concessions (available via the AfCFTA Secretariat or tralac.org resource library) and cite the specific HS chapter/heading for rice (typically HS 1006) directly.
