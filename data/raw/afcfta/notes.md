## 1. AfCFTA Tariff Concession Schedule Structure (Continental Framework)

The AfCFTA Protocol on Trade in Goods establishes three tariff liberalization categories, applied continent-wide (not Nigeria-specific figures — these are the treaty-level modalities that Nigeria, as a signatory, operates under):

- **Category A ("non-sensitive")**: ~90% of tariff lines, liberalized linearly over **5 years** for non-LDCs (10 years for LDCs).
  — tralac, "African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) FAQs," updated May 2025, https://www.tralac.org/documents/resources/faqs/5889-afcfta-frequently-asked-questions-updated-may-2025/file.html
  — Kenya Revenue Authority, "EAC Provisional Schedule of Tariff Concessions for AfCFTA – Category A Products" (confirms same 90%/5-year modality at regional level), https://www.kra.go.ke/images/publications/EAC-PROVISIONAL-SCHEDULE-OF-TARIFF-CONCESSIONS-FOR-THE-AFRICAN-CONTINENTAL-FREE-TRADE-AREA-AfCFTA-CATEGORY-A-PRODUCTS.pdf

- **Category B ("sensitive products")**: not to exceed **7%** of tariff lines; tariff dismantling starts in year 6, with non-LDCs required to fully liberalize within **10 years** total (LDCs within 13 years).
  — tralac FAQs, May 2025 (as above)
  — ITRC (International Trade & Regulatory Compliance blog), "Nigeria Gazettes AfCFTA Tariff Concessions: What Businesses and Policymakers Need to Know," 16 April 2025, https://it-rc.org/2025/04/16/nigeria-gazettes-afcfta-tariff-concessions-what-businesses-and-policymakers-need-to-know/ (confirms Category B phase-down beginning 1 January 2026 in some national schedules — note this is a country-schedule-specific start date cited in secondary reporting, not independently verified against a primary AfCFTA Secretariat document)

- **Category C ("exclusion list")**: not to exceed **3%** of tariff lines, permanently or long-term excluded from liberalization; combined intra-Africa import value of excluded lines capped at **10%** of total intra-African imports. Justifications permitted: food security, national security, fiscal revenue, livelihood protection, and industrialization.
  — tralac FAQs, May 2025 (as above)
  — WebSearch synthesis of AfCFTA Protocol on Trade in Goods provisions, corroborated across tralac and Kenya Revenue Authority sources, July 2026

**GAP / caveat**: I could not locate a Nigeria-specific published breakdown stating Nigeria's own percentages for Categories A/B/C (e.g., "Nigeria has committed X% to Category A, Y% to B, Z% to C"). Multiple secondary sources (ITRC, ThisDayLive, Economic Confidential) report only the headline "90% of tariff lines" figure for Nigeria, which appears to be a restatement of the continental modality rather than a confirmed Nigeria-specific tabulation. Nigeria's actual line-by-line Provisional Schedule of Tariff Concessions (PSTC) — the primary document that would show the true percentage split — does not appear to be freely available online in a form I could access; it is understood to be filed with the AfCFTA Secretariat and Nigeria's official gazette (Federal Government of Nigeria Official Gazette), but I did not find a direct link to the gazetted document itself.

## 2. Nigeria's Product Classifications: Petroleum, Automobiles, Rice/Agriculture

### Rice and agriculture — CONFIRMED as sensitive/excluded
Nigeria has placed core agricultural staples on its exclusion/sensitivity list under AfCFTA:
- **Rice** (semi-milled or wholly milled, polished or glazed), **wheat/cocoyam flour** (meal and powder), **sugar**, and **cement** are reported as being on Nigeria's ~184-product exclusion list, chosen on the basis of "national priorities, trade volume, priority, food security and competitive advantage."
  — The Guardian Nigeria, "Rice, flour, sugar, cement top free trade exemption list" (accessed via search synthesis; direct fetch blocked by site, 403), https://guardian.ng/business-services/rice-flour-sugar-cement-top-free-trade-exemption-list/
  — Jump.AFRICA News, "Rice, flour, sugar, concrete top streamlined commerce exclusion list" (secondary corroboration of same figures), https://jump.africa/news/rice-flour-sugar-concrete-top-streamlined-commerce-exclusion-list
- This is consistent with Nigeria's broader, longstanding non-AfCFTA-specific trade policy: rice has been subject to import prohibition from certain neighboring countries since 2004 (expanded 2016) and a ~70% duty on rice from other origins.
  — IFPRI (International Food Policy Research Institute) blog, Antoine Bouët, David Laborde, Fousseini Traoré, Swati Malhotra, "Walk the Talk: Nigeria's import prohibitions, smuggling, and the African Continental Free Trade Agreement," 20 December 2019, https://www.ifpri.org/blog/walk-talk-nigerias-import-prohibitions-smuggling-and-african-continental-free-trade-agreement/ (NOTE: this source predates the 2023-2025 window but is the clearest documented baseline for the rice tariff/prohibition regime that AfCFTA classification choices build on)
- Confirms Nigeria "remains largely protectionist in agriculture, shielding staples like rice, sugar, and poultry behind high tariffs and import controls, citing food security" — cited in coverage synthesizing Ole Boysen's 2024 World Economy journal article (see Section 4 below) and general 2025-2026 reporting.
  — Search synthesis citing Boysen (2024) and related commentary, July 2026

**Conclusion for rice/agriculture**: Best available evidence indicates Category C (exclusion) or at minimum Category B (sensitive, long-timeline) treatment for rice and other protected staples, consistent with Nigeria's decades-long protectionist stance on these products. This is corroborated by multiple independent outlets but is not sourced to a primary, line-item AfCFTA schedule document.

### Automobiles — NOT CONFIRMED in AfCFTA schedule; strong indirect evidence of protectionist treatment
- I could **not find any source explicitly stating automobiles' AfCFTA category (A/B/C) in Nigeria's schedule.** Search results note more broadly that AfCFTA state parties, as of the available reporting, "have not yet agreed on final terms for rules of origin for important categories of goods, such as autos and textiles/apparel" — suggesting the automobile sector remains unsettled/unresolved even at the continental rules-of-origin level, not just in Nigeria's national schedule.
  — Search synthesis of AfCFTA rules-of-origin status, July 2026 (specific outlet not resolved to a single named article; treat as a general status observation requiring further primary-source confirmation)
- Nigeria's non-AfCFTA automobile tariff regime (relevant as context/comparator, since it shows the protectionist baseline against which any AfCFTA concession would cut): the **National Automotive Industry Development Plan (NAIDP)**, introduced under President Goodluck Jonathan in late 2013, imposed a 35% base tariff plus a 35% "NAIDP levy" for an effective 70% total duty on imported cars (35% on commercial vehicles), while parts for local assembly were tariffed at 0-10%.
  — Search synthesis citing trade.gov "Nigeria - Automotive Sector" and "Nigeria - Import Tariffs" country commercial guides, https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/nigeria-automotive-sector , https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/nigeria-import-tariffs
- **2020-2023 reduction**: Nigeria Customs Service reduced vehicle import duty from 35% to 20% (for both new and used vehicles) under the Finance Act, in line with National Automotive Policy. TheCable reported this change under the headline "Customs: Import duty on cars reduced to 20%."
  — TheCable, "Customs: Import duty on cars reduced to 20%," https://www.thecable.ng/customs-import-duty-on-cars-reduced-to-20/ (exact publication date not confirmed in available snippet — flagged as a gap)
- **May 2023**: Nigeria Customs Excise Duty (Variation) Amendment Order 2023, issued 8 May 2023, prohibits import of used vehicles more than 12 years old (from year of manufacture) and adjusts related taxes.
  — Search synthesis citing Nigeria Customs Excise Duty (Variation) Amendment Order, 8 May 2023 (specific outlet not individually resolved; treat as requiring primary-document confirmation)
- **Impact data point**: US exports of used cars to Nigeria fell from over $1 billion (2021) to $562 million (2023), attributed to FX scarcity plus customs tariff/tax increases.
  — Search synthesis of US export statistics, source outlet not individually resolved — likely trade.gov or US Census trade data; requires further confirmation before citing as a standalone footnote.
- **2026 policy (outside the 2023-2025 window but directly relevant as the most recent data point)**: Nigeria's 2026 fiscal policy measures (effective 1 April 2026, per ministerial circular) further cut new-vehicle duty from 20% to 10% and used-vehicle duty from 15% to 5%, while adding a new "Green Tax" (2-4%) on vehicles with engines above 2,000cc.
  — Vanguard News, "FG has cut import tariffs to 5% for used vehicles, 10% for new — Adeniyi," 2026, https://www.vanguardngr.com/2026/07/fg-has-cut-import-tariffs-to-5-for-used-vehicles-10-for-new-adeniyi/
  — The Nation, "FG cuts import duties on cars, rice, others in 2026 policy," https://thenationonlineng.net/fg-cuts-import-duties-on-cars-rice-others-in-2026-policy/ (note: this headline explicitly pairs cars AND rice as both receiving import-duty cuts in the 2026 general fiscal policy — this is Nigeria's own MFN/domestic tariff policy, distinct from and not confirmed to be the same instrument as its AfCFTA preferential schedule)

**Conclusion for automobiles**: No direct evidence found of automobiles' AfCFTA category classification in Nigeria's schedule. Indirect evidence (NAIDP's historically very high 70% effective duty, still-unsettled continental rules of origin for autos, and a pattern of unilateral MFN-tariff cuts happening outside the AfCFTA track) suggests the sector is either treated as sensitive/excluded under AfCFTA or that Nigeria's applied protection for autos operates largely independent of its AfCFTA schedule commitments. This is an inference, not a confirmed classification — flagged as a genuine research gap.

### Refined petroleum — NOT FOUND in any source
- No source located that discusses refined petroleum products' AfCFTA tariff category in Nigeria's schedule specifically.
- Context found: Nigeria's downstream petroleum sector (import/export of refined products) is regulated by the NMDPRA (Nigerian Midstream and Downstream Petroleum Regulatory Authority), not primarily through AfCFTA tariff mechanisms; despite being a major crude exporter, Nigeria imports the bulk of its refined petroleum products (a structural feature independent of AfCFTA).
  — Search synthesis citing NMDPRA official mandate, https://www.nmdpra.gov.ng/, and general trade-data commentary (OEC.world Nigeria refined petroleum trade profile, https://oec.world/en/profile/bilateral-product/refined-petroleum/reporter/nga), July 2026

**Conclusion for petroleum**: This is the weakest-evidenced of the three sectors. **I found no source — primary or secondary — stating refined petroleum's AfCFTA category classification in Nigeria's schedule.** Given Nigeria's fiscal reliance on petroleum-sector revenue and the strategic sensitivity of the downstream sector (subsidy debates, local refining push via the Dangote Refinery, etc.), it is plausible refined petroleum sits in Category B or C, but **this is speculation, not a finding**, and should not be presented as fact in the portfolio piece without further primary-source research (e.g., directly requesting/locating Nigeria's gazetted PSTC or the AfCFTA Secretariat's published schedule database).

## 3. Nigeria's Actual AfCFTA Implementation Status, 2023-2025

- **Framework signing**: Nigeria signed the AfCFTA framework agreement in **July 2019** (after initial reluctance/hesitation reportedly driven by manufacturer and labor concerns about becoming a "dumping ground").
  — Search synthesis citing NESG-related commentary and general AfCFTA background reporting, July 2026

- **Guided Trade Initiative (GTI) — launch and Nigeria's entry**:
  - GTI launched by the AfCFTA Secretariat in **2022** as a "solution-based" approach for commercially meaningful trade among state parties meeting minimum requirements (matchmaking exporters/importers). Initial 2022 shipments involved Rwanda, Cameroon, Egypt, Ghana, Kenya, Mauritius, and Tanzania.
    — trade.gov, "AfCFTA Update November 2024," https://www.trade.gov/market-intelligence/afcfta-update-november-2024
  - **Nigeria and South Africa joined the GTI in the first half of 2024** — described as "major African markets" joining.
    — trade.gov, "AfCFTA Update November 2024" (as above)
  - By end of October 2024, 37 of 54 AfCFTA State Parties had completed procedures to finalize/publish tariff schedules (source does not confirm whether Nigeria specifically was among the 37 as of that date).
    — trade.gov, "AfCFTA Update November 2024" (as above)
  - **By February 2025**, 10 State Parties were actively trading goods under the GTI: Ghana, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, Mauritius, Egypt, Cameroon, South Africa, and **Nigeria** (10th not separately named in the snippet retrieved).
    — Search synthesis of GTI status reporting, February 2025 reference point, July 2026
  - GTI's conclusion/formalization was addressed at the **16th AfCFTA Council of Ministers Meeting, April 2025**, held in Kinshasa.
    — ThisDayLive, "In Major Advance for AfCFTA, Nigeria Signs ECOWAS Tariff Offers for Trade in Goods," https://www.thisdaylive.com/2025/04/15/in-major-advance-for-afcfta-nigeria-signs-ecowas-tariff-offers-for-trade-in-goods/ (NOTE: WebFetch tool returned an anomalous "July 2026" publication-date artifact for this article; the URL slug and content clearly indicate an original publication date of **15 April 2025** — treating 15 April 2025 as the correct date and flagging the tool artifact)

- **Gazetting of Nigeria's tariff schedule — DATE CONFLICT FLAGGED**:
  - ITRC blog (16 April 2025) and ThisDayLive (15 April 2025) both describe Nigeria as having gazetted/signed and transmitted its Provisional Schedule of Tariff Concessions around **mid-April 2025**, becoming the **23rd AfCFTA State Party** to do so.
    — ITRC, 16 April 2025 (as above); ThisDayLive, 15 April 2025 (as above)
  - However, a separate tralac news search result states Nigeria "gazetted the ECOWAS Provisional Schedule of Tariff Concessions on **12 September 2025**."
    — Search synthesis referencing tralac Daily News, https://www.tralac.org/news/article/17076-tralac-daily-news-13-april-2026.html (NOTE: this tralac article's own URL/date stamp reads "13 April 2026," which is itself inconsistent with a "12 September 2025" gazetting date it purportedly reports — this discrepancy could not be resolved with the tools available and should be treated as **unverified**; the April 2025 date (corroborated by two independent contemporaneous outlets, ITRC and ThisDayLive) is more likely correct, but I flag this explicitly rather than silently picking one.)
  - **Recommendation for the portfolio piece**: cite April 2025 as the gazetting date but note in a footnote that at least one source places it in September 2025, and that the primary AfCFTA Secretariat schedule-tracker (https://afcfta.au.int/en/schedules-tariff-concessions) should be checked directly for the authoritative date before final publication.

- **WTO corroboration**: Nigeria's 6th WTO Trade Policy Review took place **13 and 15 November 2024**. WTO Secretariat/member discussion noted Nigeria has bound only **19.7%** of its tariff lines, with an average bound rate of **120%**, while the average *applied* rate was **12.8% in 2023**. Members flagged that Nigeria's longstanding agricultural import prohibitions, tariff peaks, and additional levies "had the potential to contribute to food insecurity, higher food price inflation, and negatively affect private-sector investment in agriculture." The review also references Nigeria's National Action Committee structure for accelerating AfCFTA implementation, and notes Nigeria gazetted its Provisional Schedule of Tariff Concessions in **April 2025** (post-dating the review itself, so likely referenced in a follow-up/updated WTO summary rather than the review proceedings themselves — flagged as needing primary-document confirmation).
  — WTO, "Trade Policy Review – Nigeria 2024," https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/tpr_e/tp562_e.htm and "Concluding Remarks by the Chairperson," https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/tpr_e/tp562_crc_e.htm (NOTE: direct WebFetch of these pages returned only navigational/structural content, not the full text; the specific figures above come from web-search snippet synthesis and should be verified against the primary Secretariat/Government reports before final citation.)

- **Trade outcome data**: Nigeria's intra-African trade rose from **$7.47 billion (2024)** to **$9.02 billion (2025)**, a **21%** increase, attributed in reporting to AfCFTA-related policy reforms (cited alongside Afreximbank commentary).
  — allAfrica.com, "Nigeria's Intra-African Trade Up 21% to $9.02bn As AfCFTA Opens New Export Markets," https://allafrica.com/stories/202606240019.html
  — Leadership.ng, "Policy Reforms, AfCFTA Boost Nigeria's Intra-African Trade To $9bn – Afreximbank," https://leadership.ng/policy-reforms-afcfta-boost-nigerias-intra-african-trade-to-9bn-afreximbank/
  — Vanguard News, "AfCFTA lifts Nigeria's intra-African trade by 21% to $9.02bn in 2025," https://www.vanguardngr.com/2026/06/afcfta-lifts-nigerias-intra-african-trade-by-21-to-9-02bn-in-2025/
  (Note: top exports in this trade flow were reported as crude oil, chemicals, plastics/rubber, processed agricultural goods, urea, and cement — rice and automobiles were not mentioned as significant intra-African export categories for Nigeria.)

- **Implementation friction**: Despite formal GTI participation, "many Nigerian firms still face hurdles in certification, export documentation, standards compliance and market-access procedures." The **Supporting Investment and Trade in Africa (SITA)** programme is cited as instrumental in Nigeria's GTI participation, including funding evidence-based economic-impact assessments, stakeholder engagement, and technical assistance for gazetting the tariff schedule.
  — Search synthesis citing ODI (Overseas Development Institute) publication "Nigeria's participation in the Guided Trade Initiative: an economic assessment" (direct WebFetch blocked, 403 Forbidden — findings here are from the search-result snippet only, not the full report; the full ODI report should be sourced independently at https://odi.org/en/publications/nigerias-participation-in-the-guided-trade-initiative-an-economic-assessment/ for the portfolio piece since it likely contains the most rigorous quantitative economic modeling available on this topic)

## 4. Commentary and Tension: AfCFTA Commitments vs. Protectionist Tools

- **Academic source (strongest available)**: Ole Boysen, "The AfCFTA tariff offers: Current state and first revelations about members' stances towards openness and protectionism," *The World Economy*, 2024 (Wiley Online Library / also released as a JRC European Commission working paper). The paper examines submitted AfCFTA tariff offers to assess signatories' balance between fostering intra-African trade (openness) versus protecting domestic industry and tariff revenue (protectionism), and finds "significant data gaps and inconsistencies" between AfCFTA's liberalization modalities and actual trade classification standards used in offers. Nigeria specifically is characterized as committing to scrap duties on 90% of AfCFTA-traded goods while "remain[ing] largely protectionist in agriculture, shielding staples like rice, sugar, and poultry behind high tariffs and import controls, citing food security."
  — Ole Boysen, *The World Economy*, 2024, https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/twec.13580 ; working-paper version at European Commission Joint Research Centre repository, https://publications.jrc.ec.europa.eu/repository/handle/JRC136052
  — **NOTE**: I was not able to fetch the full text of this paper (paywalled on Wiley); the characterization above is drawn from search-engine snippet synthesis of the abstract/summary, not the full paper. This should be verified against the actual abstract/full text before using as a direct quotation in the portfolio piece.

- **NESG (Nigerian Economic Summit Group)**: NESG has published an "Impact Assessment Study and Economy-Wide Implications of the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) on the Nigerian Economy," and separately notes that Nigeria's initial reluctance to sign AfCFTA (pre-2019) stemmed from concerns — including from manufacturers and labor — that AfCFTA could become "a free transfer of resources arrangement from one economy to the other" rather than a genuine free-trade area. NESG recommends Nigeria diversify trade partnerships, strengthen manufacturing capability, and support local industry while deepening AfCFTA-based intra-African trade.
  — NESG, "Impact Assessment Study and Economy-Wide Implications of the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) on the Nigerian Economy," https://nesgroup.org/blog/Impact-Assessment-Study-and-Economy-Wide-Implications-of-the-African-Continental-Free-Trade-Area-(AfCFTA)-on-the-Nigerian-Economy (full text not fetched — title and general framing only, from search synthesis)

- **MAN (Manufacturers Association of Nigeria)**: MAN's most prominent 2025 public commentary relates to **US reciprocal tariffs** (a 14% tariff on Nigerian exports announced/discussed in April 2025) rather than AfCFTA-internal tensions per se, but MAN explicitly frames the US tariff shock as undermining Nigeria's AfCFTA-linked "export diversification" agenda. MAN also cites structural competitiveness problems (high energy costs, poor infrastructure, limited finance access) as bigger constraints on Nigerian manufacturers realizing AfCFTA benefits than the tariff schedule itself.
  — Tribune Online, "New tariff may stall Nigeria's N55trn budget ambition, compromise AfCFTA plans, MAN warns," https://tribuneonlineng.com/new-tariff-may-stall-nigerias-n55trn-budget-ambition-compromise-afcfta-plans-man-warns/
  — Vanguard News, "Trump's tariff has implications for Nigerian manufacturers — MAN DG," April 2025, https://www.vanguardngr.com/2025/04/trumps-tariff-has-implications-for-nigerian-manufacturers-man-dg/
  — BusinessDay NG, "Nigerian manufacturers say rising cost impedes benefits of AfCFTA," https://businessday.ng/real-sector/article/nigerian-manufacturers-say-rising-cost-impedes-benefits-of-afcfta/
  — **Note**: I did not find a MAN statement specifically addressing tension between AfCFTA tariff commitments and Nigeria's own import bans/protectionist tools in the rice, auto, or petroleum sectors specifically — this is a gap. MAN's public commentary in the sources found is more focused on US tariffs and general cost-of-doing-business complaints than an AfCFTA-vs-domestic-protectionism critique.

- **IFPRI (International Food Policy Research Institute)**: The clearest, most explicit statement found that Nigeria's unilateral trade-restriction actions "clearly go against both the letter and the spirit of AfCFTA" — referring specifically to Nigeria's August 2019 border closure against Benin, Niger, and Cameroon (blocking live poultry, pork, beef, refined vegetable oils, sugar, medications, used automobiles, and rice) and noting Nigeria's failure to formally notify ECOWAS partners of the restrictions as required. This IFPRI analysis predates the 2023-2025 window (published 20 December 2019) but is the single strongest documented "tension" source located, explicitly naming **used automobiles** and **rice** among the restricted product categories.
  — Antoine Bouët, David Laborde, Fousseini Traoré, Swati Malhotra, IFPRI Blog, 20 December 2019, https://www.ifpri.org/blog/walk-talk-nigerias-import-prohibitions-smuggling-and-african-continental-free-trade-agreement/

- **WTO members (2024 Trade Policy Review)**: Multiple WTO members, in the November 2024 review, explicitly linked Nigeria's "longstanding import prohibitions on a wide array of agricultural products," combined with "tariff peaks and additional levies," to risks of food insecurity, higher food-price inflation, and reduced private investment in agriculture — an implicit critique of the tension between Nigeria's protectionist agricultural policy and its trade-liberalization commitments (including AfCFTA).
  — WTO, Trade Policy Review – Nigeria 2024, November 2024, https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/tpr_e/tp562_e.htm (figures/framing from search-snippet synthesis; full report not directly fetched)

- **General framing (FrontierView / trade advisory commentary)**: "Nigeria's trade policy will stay protectionist" is presented as a standing assessment by trade-risk advisory firm FrontierView, reinforcing the broader "AfCFTA commitment vs. protectionist practice" tension identified across sources.
  — FrontierView, "Nigeria's trade policy will stay protectionist," https://frontierview.com/insights/nigerias-trade-policy-will-stay-protectionist/ (title/framing only; full text not fetched)
