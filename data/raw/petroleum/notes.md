## 1. Import Tariffs/Duties on Refined Petroleum Products (PMS, AGO/diesel, kerosene)

### Baseline position 2023–late 2025: no special petroleum import duty
- Throughout most of 2023–2025, Nigeria did **not** operate a dedicated ad-valorem import duty specifically targeting PMS (petrol) or AGO (diesel) imports; these largely moved under the general ECOWAS Common External Tariff (CET) framework administered by Nigeria Customs Service, which Nigeria migrated to an updated version of in April 2022 — Nigeria Customs Service CET pages, accessed July 2026, https://customs.gov.ng/?page_id=3133. **I could not find a specific, verified CET percentage rate for PMS/AGO/kerosene tariff lines** in the sources reviewed; this is a gap (see Gaps section).
- Finance Act 2023 (signed into law 28 May 2023) amended the Customs, Excise Tariff, etc. (Consolidation) Act (CETA) to introduce a **0.5% levy on all eligible goods imported into Nigeria from outside Africa** (to fund Nigeria's capital contributions/subscriptions to multilateral institutions such as the African Union, ECOWAS, African Development Bank) — EY, "Nigeria | Highlights of Finance Act 2023," https://www.ey.com/en_gl/technical/tax-alerts/nigeria---highlights-of-finance-act-2023, accessed July 2026; KPMG, "Tax News Flash," 14 July 2023, https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/us/pdf/2023/07/tnf-nigeria-july14-2023.pdf. This levy is general (not petroleum-specific), but would technically apply to non-African-sourced refined product imports.
- The Finance Act 2023 also amended Section 21 of CETA to introduce **excise duties on services** (e.g., telecoms), and aligned the Petroleum Profits Tax Act (PPTA) with the Petroleum Industry Act (PIA) 2021 terminology (defining "Commission" as the Nigerian Upstream Petroleum Regulatory Commission) — Lexology, "Petroleum Profits Tax Act, Petroleum Industry Act and proposed amendments in Finance Act," accessed July 2026, https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=f6b0bc1d-d5b5-47fd-83c5-ebb70f908d60. **No petroleum-products-specific import tariff change was identified in Finance Act 2023 itself.**
- I did not find a distinct, well-documented "Finance Act 2024" petroleum-import-tariff provision in the sources reviewed (searches returned mostly VAT Modification Order 2024 material on gas equipment, not PMS/AGO/kerosene). This is a gap — flagged below.
- Separately, in January 2024 the Federal Government (via Finance Act/VAT Modification Order mechanisms) **waived VAT and Customs duties on gas and gas equipment** (LPG/CNG-related, not PMS/diesel/kerosene) — PwC Nigeria Tax & Business Matters blog, January 2024, https://pwcnigeria.typepad.com/tax_matters_nigeria/2024/01/fg-waives-vat-and-customs-duties-waiver-on-gas-and-gas-equipment.html.

### The 15% ad-valorem import duty episode (October–November 2025)
This is the single most significant, well-documented petroleum-specific import-tariff action in the 2023–2025 window, though it falls at the very end of the period and was short-lived.

- **21 October 2025**: President Bola Tinubu approved a **15% ad-valorem import duty on imported PMS (petrol) and AGO (diesel)**, applied to the Cost, Insurance and Freight (CIF) value of imported cargoes. Approval was conveyed via the president's private secretary following a request from the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS); directed to FIRS, NMDPRA, and Nigeria Customs Service for implementation. Legal basis cited: Section 13 of the Customs, Excise Tariffs, etc. (Consolidation) Act, which empowers the president to impose, amend or vary import/excise duty. The policy was officially titled the **"Market-Responsive Tariffs Framework."** — Nairametrics, "Tinubu approves 15% import duty on diesel, petrol imports," 30 October 2025, https://nairametrics.com/2025/10/30/tinubu-approves-15-import-duty-on-diesel-petrol-imports/; Punch, "Tinubu approves 15% import duty on petrol, diesel," 30 October 2025, https://punchng.com/just-in-tinubu-approves-15-import-duty-on-petrol-diesel/; Sahara Reporters, 30 October 2025, https://saharareporters.com/2025/10/30/exclusive-tinubu-government-approves-15-fuel-import-duty-moves-give-dangote-refinery.
- **Stated rationale**: FIRS Chairman Zacch Adedeji said the tariff would help Nigeria achieve fuel self-sufficiency, protect consumers/investors, and stabilise the downstream petroleum market; government framing was to "align fuel import pricing with domestic production and protect local refineries," referencing the July 29, 2024 Federal Executive Council approval that allowed crude oil and refined products to be traded in naira — ICIR Nigeria, "FG suspends 15% import duty on petrol, diesel," accessed July 2026, https://www.icirnigeria.org/fg-suspends-15-import-duty-on-petrol-diesel/.
- **Estimated price impact**: implementation was projected to raise the price of a litre of petrol by approximately **N99.72 kobo** — Nairametrics, 30 October 2025 (as above).
- **Transition window**: a 30-day transition period was granted (to allow importers to adjust cargoes already in transit), due to end **21 November 2025** — Mondaq/KPMG-affiliated legal update, "Nigeria Introduces 15% Import Duty On Diesel And Petroleum Products," 13 November 2025, https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/international-trade-investment/1704694/nigeria-introduces-15-import-duty-on-diesel-and-petroleum-products-what-stakeholders-need-to-know.
- **Suspension**: on **13 November 2025** (statement date; decision reported as of 12–13 November), the NMDPRA (via Director of Public Affairs George Ene-Ita, posted on its X/Twitter handle) announced the implementation was **"no longer in view,"** following pushback from oil marketers, energy experts, and civil society who warned it would sharply raise pump prices — ICIR Nigeria, as above; Ecofin Agency / Africanews, "Nigeria scraps planned 15% import duty on petrol and diesel," 13 November 2025, https://www.africanews.com/amp/2025/11/13/nigeria-scraps-planned-15-import-duty-on-petrol-and-diesel/.
- Tinubu reportedly instructed NMDPRA to develop clear guidelines requiring domestic production to be prioritized before import permits are granted, and ordered periodic re-evaluation of the tariff's relevance/rate with a possible future phase-in as local refining capacity grows — Nairametrics, 30 October 2025 (as above).
- **Net effect**: the 15% duty was never actually implemented/collected — it was approved, then suspended before its effective date. This shows the tariff was used more as a policy lever/signal (favoring Dangote and other local refiners) than an implemented fiscal measure during the 2023–2025 window strictly defined.

## 2. Petroleum Subsidy Removal (May 2023) and Interaction with Import Policy

- **29 May 2023**: President Bola Tinubu declared in his inauguration speech, **"the Fuel Subsidy is gone!"** — Al Jazeera, "Nigeria fuel subsidy cut and spiralling costs: What to know," 31 May 2023, https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/5/31/nigeria-fuel-subsidy-cut-spiralling-costs-all-you-need-to-know.
- **31 May 2023**: NNPC Limited adjusted retail PMS prices nationally, to a range of **N488/litre (Lagos) to N555/litre (Maiduguri, Borno State)** — Al Jazeera, as above.
- Pre-removal official pump price was around **N185/litre (~$0.40)**; post-removal prices were expected to range **N350–N550/litre (~$0.76–$1.18)** — Al Jazeera, as above.
- KPMG Nigeria's analysis frames the subsidy as expenditure on inefficient consumption that "exceeded the combined expenditure on health, education and infrastructure" and was not effectively reaching intended beneficiaries — KPMG Nigeria, "Removing Nigeria's PMS Fuel Subsidies," June 2023, https://kpmg.com/ng/en/home/insights/2023/06/removing-nigeria-pms-fuel-subsidies.html; full PDF: https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/removing-nigerias-pms-fuel-subsidies23.pdf.

### Fiscal savings claims
- Nigeria's Finance Minister claimed the country had saved **no less than N20 trillion** since subsidy removal (cumulative claim, exact "as of" date not specified in source found) — cited in TheCable, "Three years on: What petrol subsidy removal has given — and taken," accessed July 2026, https://www.thecable.ng/three-years-on-what-petrol-subsidy-removal-has-given-and-taken/.
- Presidency claim: subsidy removal was saving Nigeria approximately **$7.5 billion annually** as of late 2024 (≈N12 trillion at prevailing exchange rates per the reporting) — Nairametrics, "Fuel subsidy removal saves Nigeria $7.5 billion annually — Presidency," 25 November 2024, https://nairametrics.com/2024/11/25/fuel-subsidy-removal-saves-nigeria-7-5-billion-annually-presidency/.
- Prior to removal, the FG had disbursed **N3.6 trillion** on subsidy payments (period unspecified in source, likely partial 2023), with projections that continuing the subsidy could have cost **N5.4 trillion in 2024**, rising to as much as **N6.3 trillion** if unchecked — cited in TheCable / Guardian coverage, accessed July 2026, https://guardian.ng/news/despite-n7tr-subsidy-savings-states-fail-to-cushion-hardship-hunger/.
- FAAC (Federation Account Allocation Committee) monthly disbursements to federal/state/local governments reportedly rose from **N16.28 trillion (2023, full year)** to **N28.78 trillion (2024, full year)**; monthly FAAC allocations rose from roughly **N760 billion (2023)** to **N3.2 trillion (2024)** — cited in TheCable, as above. **Caveat: these FAAC increases reflect combined effects of subsidy removal, naira devaluation (inflating naira-denominated oil revenue), and other reforms — they should not be read as a "subsidy savings" figure in isolation.**
- World Bank estimates of cumulative subsidy-removal fiscal benefit are cited in the range of **N11 trillion to N20 trillion**, heavily caveated by the effect of naira depreciation — cited in TheCable, as above. **This is a wide range and should be flagged as imprecise/contested in any write-up.**

### Subsidy reintroduction / "under-recovery" (2024)
Important nuance for a rigorous case study: subsidy removal was not a clean one-way policy — de facto subsidy-like support returned within about a year, mediated through NNPC's balance sheet rather than official policy:
- Nigeria **reintroduced a petrol subsidy in August 2024** in practice, with the federal government reportedly owing NNPC **N7.8 trillion (~$4.9 billion)** in subsidy-related debt accumulated January–July 2024 — Legit.ng, "FG reintroduces fuel subsidy, to pay NNPC trillions of naira debt from projected revenue," accessed July 2026, https://www.legit.ng/business-economy/economy/1609175-fg-reintroduces-fuel-subsidy-pay-nnpc-trillions-naira-debt-projected-revenue/.
- NNPC initially denied reintroducing a subsidy, then confirmed related payments — TheCable, "After denying subsidy reintroduction, NNPC confirms payments," accessed July 2026, https://www.thecable.ng/after-denying-subsidy-reintroduction-nnpc-confirms-payments/.
- Pump price timeline (Abuja, NNPC-set):
  - September 2024: **N897/litre** (per NNPC's estimated pricing tied to Dangote-refined PMS) — NNPC Group, "NNPC Ltd Releases Estimated Pump Prices of PMS from Dangote Refinery, Based on September 2024 Pricing," https://nnpcgroup.com/insights/nnpc-ltd-releases-estimated-pump-prices-of-pms-from-dangote-refinery-based-on-september-2024-pricing, accessed July 2026.
  - October 2024 (after full deregulation): raised to **N1,030/litre in Abuja, N998/litre in Lagos** — ThisDay, "Finally, Subsidy Is Gone! NNPC Sells Petrol for N1,030/Litre in Abuja, N998 in Lagos," 10 October 2024, https://www.thisdaylive.com/2024/10/10/finally-subsidy-is-gone-nnpc-sells-petrol-for-n1030-litre-in-abuja-n998-in-lagos/.
  - More recent (2025, exact date not specified in source found): NNPC cut pump price to **N895/litre (Abuja), N865/litre (Lagos)**, attributed to competitive market dynamics post-deregulation — The Guardian Nigeria, "NNPC cuts petrol price to ₦895 in Abuja, ₦865 in Lagos," accessed July 2026, https://guardian.ng/energy/nnpc-cuts-petrol-price-to-%E2%82%A6895-in-abuja-%E2%82%A6865-in-lagos/.

## 3. Dangote Refinery: Timeline, Capacity vs. Output, and Import-Protection Debate

### Timeline
- **22 May 2023**: Dangote Refinery officially inaugurated/commissioned — Wikipedia, "Dangote refinery," accessed July 2026, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dangote_refinery (cross-referenced against multiple news sources; treat Wikipedia as a secondary aggregator, not a primary citation — see sources.md note).
- **January 2024**: Refinery began operations, initially processing crude into products such as diesel and aviation fuel (jet fuel) — per multiple secondary sources; exact date within January 2024 not confirmed.
- **3 September 2024**: Refinery announced start of PMS (petrol) production — Vanguard, "Dangote Refinery begins petrol production, vows to ease Nigeria's fuel crisis," September 2024, https://www.vanguardngr.com/2024/09/breaking-dangote-refinery-rolls-out-petrol/.
  - Initial expected September 2024 output: **25 million litres of petrol/day**.
  - Announced ramp-up: **35 million litres/day from October 2024** — Legit.ng, "Year in Review: Dangote, PH Refineries PMS Production," accessed July 2026, https://www.legit.ng/business-economy/energy/1633395-year-review-dangote-ph-refineries-pms-production-major-events-nigerias-petroleum-sector/.
  - NNPC paid Dangote in **USD** for September 2024 PMS offtake; **naira-denominated transactions began 1 October 2024** — Nairametrics, "Dangote refinery: Naira transaction for PMS to begin October 1st – NNPC," 16 September 2024, https://nairametrics.com/2024/09/16/dangote-refinery-naira-transaction-for-pms-to-begin-october-1st-nnpc/.

### Capacity vs. actual output
- **Nameplate/design capacity: 650,000 barrels per day (bpd)** — cited consistently across sources, e.g. Intelpoint, "Dangote Refinery led with a capacity of 650,000 barrels per day...," accessed July 2026, https://intelpoint.co/insights/dangote-refinery-led-with-a-capacity-of-650000-barrels-per-day-representing-almost-67-of-nigerias-total-functional-refining-capacity-in-early-2025/ (this source also states Dangote represented **~67% of Nigeria's total functional refining capacity in early 2025**).
- **Q1 2025 (specific month not stated)**: refinery reported operating at **about 85% of its 650,000 bpd capacity** — Nairametrics, 12 June 2025, https://nairametrics.com/2025/06/12/nigerias-petrol-import-bill-crashes-by-54-in-q1-2025-as-dangote-boosts-local-supply/.
- **August 2025**: output reported at **610,000 bpd** — per WithinNigeria aggregator piece, accessed July 2026, https://www.withinnigeria.com/2026/04/23/dangote-crude-oil-production-output-figures-milestones-and-what-it-means-for-nigerias-economy/ (note: this is a secondary/aggregator source published 2026; treat with some caution and corroborate before using in a polished deliverable).
- **February 2026** (just outside the core 2023–2025 window but useful trend context): refinery reported to have hit **full refining capacity**, and separately reported to have **raised processing capacity to 700,000 bpd**, i.e. above nameplate — Premium Times, "Dangote refinery raises processing capacity to 700,000 barrels per day," https://www.premiumtimesng.com/business/business-news/885157-dangote-refinery-raises-processing-capacity-to-700000-barrels-per-day.html; Nairametrics, 4 June 2026, https://nairametrics.com/2026/06/04/dangote-refinery-raises-crude-processing-capacity-to-700000-barrels-per-day/; Business Post Nigeria, "Dangote Refinery Finally Hits Full 650,000-Barrel Per Day Capacity," https://businesspost.ng/economy/dangote-refinery-finally-hits-full-650000-barrel-per-day-capacity/.
- **October 2025**: Aliko Dangote announced expansion plans targeting **1.4 million bpd** capacity (future expansion, not 2023-2025 actual) — per WithinNigeria/CEN aggregator coverage, accessed July 2026.
- PMS market share (daily domestic supply): **92% of Nigeria's daily petrol supply in February 2026** attributed to Dangote — Punch, "Dangote Petrol Supply Hits 92% as Nigeria Halts Imports," accessed July 2026, https://punchng.com/dangote-now-supplies-92-of-petrol-as-fg-pauses-imports/. February 2026 NMDPRA fact sheet also cited an average of **36.5 million litres/day** supplied by Dangote that month — per Bloomberg-sourced reporting (see below).

### NNPC–Dangote "Naira-for-Crude" deal
- **July 29, 2024**: Federal Executive Council approval enabling crude oil and refined products to be traded in naira (legal/policy basis for the naira-for-crude framework) — referenced in ICIR Nigeria coverage of the 15% duty policy, https://www.icirnigeria.org/fg-suspends-15-import-duty-on-petrol-diesel/, accessed July 2026.
- Initial naira-for-crude agreement was structured as a **six-month contract**, which **expired 31 March 2025**; renegotiation followed — ThisDayLive, "Naira-for-Crude Deal: We are Renegotiating, Not Cancelling Agreement, Says NNPC," 11 March 2025, https://www.thisdaylive.com/index.php/2025/03/11/naira-for-crude-policy-remains-in-place-committee-chair/; Vanguard, "Naira for Crude: NNPC deal with Dangote remains intact, says FG," March 2025, https://www.vanguardngr.com/2025/03/naira-for-crude-nnpc-deal-with-dangote-remains-intact-says-fg/.
- **August 2025**: NNPC signed a **fresh two-year crude supply agreement** with Dangote Refinery, to ensure steady feedstock to the 650,000 bpd Lekki plant, prioritizing naira-denominated crude deliveries — Businessday NG, "NNPC, Dangote Refinery ink new two-year crude supply deal," accessed July 2026, https://businessday.ng/energy/article/nnpc-dangote-refinery-ink-new-two-year-crude-supply-deal/; Punch, "NNPC-Dangote Refinery sign fresh two-year crude deal," accessed July 2026, https://punchng.com/nnpc-dangote-refinery-sign-fresh-two-year-crude-deal/.
- **Volume allocated under naira-for-crude, Oct 2024–Oct 2025**: **82 million barrels total** allocated to Dangote Refinery, of which **60% (49.3 million barrels)** was supplied in naira — per Authority News, 30 September 2025, https://authorityngr.com/2025/09/30/nnpc-renews-crude-oil-contract-deal-with-dangote/ (figure attributed to NNPC/reporting on the renewal).
- **Deal strain (late 2025–2026, outside core window but material to sustainability of the arrangement)**: reported crude oil supply shortfall of approximately **79.53 million barrels between October 2025 and mid-March 2026**; Dangote reportedly received only **5 cargoes/month from NNPC versus 13 cargoes required**, with the shortfall cargoes paid for at international market prices rather than the naira arrangement — Punch, "Dangote-NNPC Deal Falters Amid Massive Crude Oil Shortfall," accessed July 2026, https://punchng.com/dangote-nnpc-deal-hits-turbulence-over-crude-shortfall/.

### Import protection debate and eventual import restriction
- Under President Tinubu's **"Nigeria First" policy** (launched 2024), government agencies are barred from buying foreign goods when equivalent local goods are available — cited in Tekedia, "Dangote Urges Tinubu to Ban Fuel Imports Under 'Nigeria First' Policy...," accessed July 2026, https://www.tekedia.com/dangote-urges-tinubu-to-ban-fuel-imports-under-nigeria-first-policy-sparks-fresh-monopoly-allegations-pushback-from-marketers-experts/.
- Aliko Dangote publicly and repeatedly **urged a full ban on refined-product imports**, arguing much imported fuel was "cheap, often toxic" and substandard, and that some importers brought in fuel subsidized abroad (e.g., Russia), undercutting local refiners' economics — OilPrice.com, "Dangote Demands Fuel Import Ban to Protect Nigeria's Refining Future," accessed July 2026, https://oilprice.com/Latest-Energy-News/World-News/Dangote-Demands-Fuel-Import-Ban-to-Protect-Nigerias-Refining-Future.html.
- **Independent marketers/oil marketing companies opposed an outright ban**, citing monopoly risk and loss of competitive price discipline — Punch/Danco Group coverage, accessed July 2026, https://dancogroup.com.ng/fuel-war-brews-as-dangote-presses-tinubu-to-ban-imports/.
- **February 2026 NMDPRA "State of the Midstream and Downstream" Fact Sheet**: disclosed that **no fresh PMS import licences were issued in February 2026**. NMDPRA spokesperson George Ene-Ita: *"At this moment, there is no need to import because local production is meeting supply. When there is a shortfall, we will issue licensing to buffer local production."* This approach was framed as consistent with the Petroleum Industry Act (PIA), under which imports are meant to serve as a buffer only when domestic supply is insufficient — Economy Post, "NMDPRA halts new petrol import licences as local production meets demand," 11 March 2026, https://economypost.ng/oil-and-gas/nmdpra-halts-new-petrol-import-licences-as-local-production-meets-demand/2026/03/11/; Enerdata, "Nigeria suspends gasoline import licences," accessed July 2026, https://www.enerdata.net/publications/daily-energy-news/nigeria-suspends-gasoline-import-licences.html; Bloomberg, "Nigeria Halts Gasoline Import Permits in a Win for Dangote," 10 March 2026, https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/nigeria-halts-gasoline-import-permits-in-a-win-for-dangote (headline/framing captured via search-result summary; full article text was blocked — HTTP 403 — when fetched directly, so treat direct quotes from this particular outlet as unverified beyond the headline/snippet).
- Firms affected by the halt on import licences reportedly included a unit of **TotalEnergies SE, Conoil Plc, and MRS Nigeria Plc**, which together had imported **38% of the nation's gasoline in January (2026)** — per Bloomberg-sourced reporting captured in search results, corroborated by Economy Post, as above.
- **Note on dispute**: as of March 2026, Dangote reportedly filed a **new lawsuit against Nigeria/NMDPRA** disputing whether import licences were, in fact, still being issued despite official statements to the contrary — CNBC Africa, "Dangote files new lawsuit against Nigeria over fuel import licences," accessed July 2026, https://www.cnbcafrica.com/2026/dangote-files-new-lawsuit-against-nigeria-over-fuel-import-licences; ThisDayLive, "Dangote: NMDPRA Still Issuing Fuel Import Licences Despite to the Contrary," 12 March 2026, https://www.thisdaylive.com/2026/03/12/dangote-nmdpra-still-issuing-fuel-import-licences-despite-to-the-contrary/. This shows the "import ban" narrative was contested/imperfectly enforced even after the announced halt — important nuance, not a clean policy win.
- **This import-license halt (March 2026) is technically just outside the 2023–2025 window specified**, but is included here as essential trend context showing where the 2023-2025 dynamics (Dangote ramp-up + policy tilt toward import substitution) were heading.

## 5. CBN Forex Policy Changes 2023–2025 and Effects on Petroleum Importers

- **June 2023**: Under President Tinubu, the CBN terminated the previous "controlled float" exchange rate system and moved toward a **unified exchange rate regime** — per search-result synthesis citing general CBN reform coverage, accessed July 2026, https://www.cbn.gov.ng/AboutCBN/Reforms.html. **Caveat: when I fetched the CBN Reforms page directly, it did not explicitly corroborate a June 2023 date — it instead documented an October 12, 2023 unification action (see below). The June 2023 date appears in secondary sources/aggregated search summaries but I was not able to confirm it against a primary CBN document in this research pass. Treat "June 2023" as reported-but-not-directly-verified; the October 12, 2023 date is the one I could verify directly from a CBN-linked source.**
- **CBN action verified directly from cbn.gov.ng**: collapse of all FX market segments into the **Investors' & Exporters' (I&E) window**, subsequently renamed the **Nigerian Foreign Exchange Market (NFEM)**, adopting a "willing buyer, willing seller" model to reduce arbitrage and improve transparency — CBN, "Reforms and Initiatives," https://www.cbn.gov.ng/AboutCBN/Reforms.html, accessed July 2026.
- **12 October 2023**: CBN lifted the restriction on sourcing foreign exchange from the official NFEM for importation of **43 items** (mostly food/agricultural products historically barred from the official FX window) — per Premium Times, "UPDATED: CBN announces unification of FX windows, abolishes Naira4Dollar scheme," https://www.premiumtimesng.com/news/headlines/604550-just-in-cbn-announces-unification-of-fx-windows-stops-naira4dollar-scheme.html, accessed July 2026 (date of the underlying policy action reported in secondary sources as 12 October 2023). **Petroleum products were not on the historical "43 items" restricted list** (that list targeted mostly agricultural/consumer goods), so this specific action's direct relevance to petroleum importers is limited — flagged as likely tangential rather than a direct petroleum-import policy lever.
- **31 May 2024**: CBN directive requiring **International Oil Companies (IOCs)** to cap export proceeds repatriation — initially retain/convert 50% of export proceeds domestically, with the remaining 50% repatriated after 90 days — per CBN Reforms page, https://www.cbn.gov.ng/AboutCBN/Reforms.html, accessed July 2026. This affects crude-export FX supply dynamics (relevant to overall NGN liquidity available for fuel imports) but is not a direct "dollar allocation to fuel importers" policy.
- **On direct dollar allocation to fuel/oil importers specifically**: the CBN **publicly denied** media reports that it had disbursed **US$1.259 billion** directly to major oil marketing operators for refined petroleum product imports. Since the June/October 2023 unification, the CBN's official position is that the NFEM operates as a market-driven system where FX is sourced/supplied by market participants, **not centrally allocated by the CBN** to any specific importer class (including fuel importers) — Zawya, "Nigeria: CBN clarifies $1.26bln forex data, denies direct allocation to oil marketers," accessed July 2026, https://www.zawya.com/en/economy/africa/nigeria-cbn-clarifies-126bln-forex-data-denies-direct-allocation-to-oil-marketers-brr49vpr. **This is an important nuance: it means CBN forex policy affected petroleum importers primarily indirectly — through overall naira depreciation/availability of FX in the market — rather than through a targeted allocation or restriction mechanism aimed at fuel imports specifically.** This also helps explain why the 2024 naira-value import-cost spike (Section 4) is largely an FX-depreciation story rather than a volume story.
- The 40.9% naira depreciation figure for 2024 (cited in Section 4 re: the N15.42tn PMS import spike) is the clearest concrete link between CBN/forex policy and petroleum import costs found in this research pass — Nairametrics, 10 March 2025, as above.
