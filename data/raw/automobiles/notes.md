## 1. Nigeria's auto tariff structure (2023-2025) and pre-2023 history

### Pre-2023 baseline (context)
- In October 2013, the Federal Government's original National Automotive Industry Development Plan (NAIDP)
  raised the combined duty+levy on imported new and used passenger cars from 20% to **70%** (35% base duty +
  35% NAIDP levy), effective in stages through 2014-2015. — Inside Privacy Blog (Covington & Burling LLP),
  Feb 1, 2016, https://natlawreview.com/article/nigeria-s-automotive-industry-shifts-high-gear;
  corroborated by allAfrica.com, Sept 22, 2014, https://allafrica.com/stories/201409222120.html
- Commercial vehicles were raised to 35% combined duty+levy (lower than the 70% for cars). — same source
  (natlawreview.com/Covington, Feb 1, 2016)
- CKD/SKD (partially-assembled) kits were given concessional rates of 5-10%, and parts imported for full
  local assembly were duty-free, to create a tariff wall favoring local assembly. — same source
- Effect: new car imports fell 67% in 2015 vs. 2014, exceeding the government's 65% reduction target.
  — natlawreview.com/Covington, Feb 1, 2016

### 2023-2025 (Tinubu administration) rates — the rates in force through this research window
- **Used ("tokunbo") vehicles:** 20% base import duty + 15% National Automotive Council (NAC) levy
  (= 35% combined, before other charges). — trade.gov Nigeria Country Commercial Guide, "Nigeria - Import
  Tariffs," https://www.trade.gov/country-commercial-guides/nigeria-import-tariffs (content reflects
  post-April-2022 rates, "retained... in line with the Finance Act and the National Automotive Policy,"
  under Annex II of the ECOWAS 2022-2026 Common External Tariff)
- **New vehicles:** 20% base import duty + 20% NAC levy (= 40% combined). — same source (trade.gov)
- **CKD kits (for bona fide licensed assemblers):** 0% duty. — search-derived summary citing Nigeria
  Customs Service tariff schedule (via WebSearch synthesis, source could not be independently re-verified
  with a single named primary document beyond the aggregated summary — flagged as a gap below)
- **SKD kits (for bona fide licensed assemblers):** 10% duty. — same as above
- **ECOWAS/CET reference rates** for CKD/SKD generally: 5% and 10% respectively under the Common External
  Tariff — cited in aggregated WebSearch results referencing the 2022-2026 CET edition; not independently
  confirmed against the primary CET document in this pass (gap).
- **Additional charges layered on top of duty+NAC levy (2023-2025):** a 4% FOB (free-on-board) value levy
  was legislated to replace the former 7% customs processing/collection fee and 1% Comprehensive Import
  Supervision Scheme (CISS) fee, under the **Nigeria Customs Service Act 2023 (NCSA 2023)**. Also cited:
  a 0.5% ETLS (ECOWAS Trade Liberalisation Scheme) levy. — WebSearch synthesis of multiple sources including
  Punch, "Customs, agents at war over 15% levy, imported car price set to soar," punchng.com; and
  research.hktdc.com, "NIGERIA: New Taxes on Imports and Updates to Banned...," https://research.hktdc.com/en/article/MTM4NjIwOTI4Nw
  - Note: implementation of the 4% FOB levy was contested and its timeline spans into 2025 (see below),
    so it should be treated as a 2024-2025 development, not one settled from 2023.
- **90-day duty regularization window:** On March 3, 2024, the Nigeria Customs Service announced a 90-day
  window allowing owners of improperly imported/under-valued vehicles to regularize payment of import duty
  (plus a **25% penalty**), under the Destination Inspection Scheme (2013) and NCSA 2023, via Zonal
  Coordinators/FCT Customs Area Controller. — Punch, "FG approves 90-day window to regularise import duty
  on vehicles," March 3, 2024, https://punchng.com/fg-approves-90-day-window-to-regularise-import-duty-on-vehicles/
  (also reported same day by Channels TV and WithinNigeria)

### 4% FOB levy timeline (2025, adjacent to but technically outside the 2023-2025 window's early years)
- Introduced February 2025, aimed at funding NCS modernization (digital systems, inspection tools);
  withdrawn, then reinstated August 2025; suspended September 17, 2025 by Finance Minister Wale Edun, who
  said "the implementation of the 4% FOB charge poses significant challenges to Nigerian trade facilitation,
  the business environment, and economic stability," amid 20.12% inflation in August 2025. — Ecofin Agency,
  "Nigeria halts 4% import tax to curb rising prices and ease business costs,"
  https://www.ecofinagency.com/news/1709-48755-nigeria-halts-4-import-tax-to-curb-rising-prices-and-ease-business-costs
  (accessed July 7, 2026; exact publication date of article not independently confirmed beyond the events
  described, which are dated Feb/Aug/Sept 2025 in the text)

## 2. Local assembly incentives and effectiveness

### Tariff differential mechanism
- The core incentive mechanism is the tariff gap between fully-built imports (40% new / 35% used, per
  Section 1 above) versus CKD (0%) / SKD (10%) kits for licensed assemblers — designed to give locally
  assembled vehicles a price advantage. — trade.gov, "Nigeria - Import Tariffs"; also referenced in
  WebSearch synthesis around "the tariff differential between fully built imported vehicles and locally
  assembled vehicles is a critical pillar of NAIDP"

### Historical capacity data (NAIDP 2014-2024 first cycle)
- **58 companies** came on stream as licensed automotive assemblers between 2014 and 2019, with a combined
  **installed capacity of over 500,000 units/year**. — The Guardian Nigeria, Benjamin Alade, March 27, 2025
  (guardian.ng/news/national-automotive-policy-in-limbo-n136b-investments-stuck/)
- Of those 58, **38 companies remained "in operation"** as of the March 2025 reporting, but only **6
  companies were "actively assembling vehicles"** at meaningful scale — "extremely low capacities" per the
  reporting. — same source
- **20 companies had suspended operations entirely**, representing **$89.6 million (≈N136 billion)** in
  stalled/stranded investment — **12% of the total $756 million** invested in the sector by the 58 firms
  over the plan's first five years (2014-2019 period, though the reporting date is 2025). — same source
  (Guardian, March 27, 2025)
- Current combined yearly capacity across the sector cited at **450,000 units** (down slightly from the
  original 500,000+ installed figure). — same source
- Quote: "Policy summersaults and half-hearted implementation are the hallmarks of the automotive policy"
  — Prof. Samuel Odewumi, Lagos State University School of Transport and Logistics, quoted in Guardian,
  March 27, 2025

### 2024 NADDC licensing update
- **New assembly plants registered during 2024 increased the number of NADDC-licensed companies to 85**,
  with the department **revalidating 23 licenses** for existing local assemblers and processing **14 new
  applications**. — WebSearch synthesis attributed to NADDC reporting/publications (naddc.gov.ng); I was
  unable to open the specific NADDC page confirming this exact figure as a directly-quotable primary
  document in this pass — treat as sourced via aggregated secondary reporting citing NADDC. **Flagged as
  a gap**: recommend directly pulling the NADDC 2024 annual report/press release for primary confirmation.
- A separate figure states **37 companies** maintained production-quality standards sufficient for annual
  license revalidation (2024) — same caveat on primary-source confirmation as above.

### Overall assessment of effectiveness
- The tariff-differential incentive has produced a large number of licensed/registered assemblers (58
  historically, growing toward 85 by end-2024) but actual capacity utilization is very low in aggregate
  (≈24% by my calculation from the April 2024 NADDC data; only 6 of 58 historic licensees described as
  "actively assembling" at scale by March 2025 reporting). This suggests many licenses may represent
  CKD/SKD "screwdriver assembly" arrangements that qualify for tariff concessions without generating
  proportionate production volume — a common critique in Nigerian trade-policy commentary, though I did
  not find a single named source making this exact synthesis; it is my own inference from the combined
  data points above and should be labeled as **analysis, not a directly sourced claim**.
- PAN Nigeria and Mikano International show the lowest utilization rates in the dataset (20% and ~7%
  respectively), while Innoson and Cedric Masters show the highest (70% and 75%).

## 3. Used vehicle ("tokunbo") import volumes and age-restriction policy history

### Age limit policy history
- The Nigeria Customs Service (NCS) **reduced the maximum importable vehicle age from 15 years to 12
  years in May 2022**, introduced alongside a new **VIN (Vehicle Identification Number) Valuation System**
  that automatically cross-references a vehicle's VIN against its model year to flag/reject/penalize
  vehicles exceeding the threshold. — TheWill News, "Customs Lowers Age Limit For Imported Vehicles From
  15 To 12 Years," https://thewillnews.com/customs-lowers-age-limit-for-imported-vehicles-from-15-to-12-years/;
  corroborated by Daily Trend, May 20, 2022, https://dailytrend.com.ng/2022/05/20/nigeria-customs-announce-12-years-age-limit-for-imported-vehicles/
- **Source conflict noted:** A separate source (WheelZAR, accessed Dec 21, 2025) states the 12-year limit
  "has been in place since 2013" and "aligns with ECOWAS standards," which directly contradicts the
  2022-transition narrative above. I judge the 2022 narrative (corroborated by two independent contemporary
  2022 news reports) to be more reliable than the single later WheelZAR retrospective claim, but flag this
  explicitly as an **unresolved source conflict** — the underlying regulatory history (was it always 12
  years, or was it lowered from 15 in 2022?) should be verified against an actual NCS circular/gazette if
  higher confidence is needed. — https://wheelzar.com/nigerias-car-import-policy-age-limits-and-customs-duties/
- In January 2023, Customs reportedly further tightened the practical cutoff, described as "switching the
  age limit of vehicles to 2014" (i.e., a rolling ~9-year effective cutoff for that valuation cycle),
  according to Daily Trend, Jan 5, 2023,
  https://dailytrend.com.ng/2023/01/05/customs-switches-age-limit-of-vehicles-to-2014-freight-forwarders-kick/
  — this suggests the NCS applies the age rule on a rolling/annually-updated basis rather than a fixed
  "12 years forever" rule, which would reconcile some of the apparent conflict above, but I did not verify
  this reconciliation with a primary NCS document. **Flagged as a gap.**
- A related but distinct claim (Businessday headline only, fetch blocked by paywall/403) references a
  "new customs valuation system pegs age limit of cars at 9 years" — title suggests a further tightening
  at some point, but I could not access article text to confirm date or reconcile with the 12-year figure
  above. **Flagged as an unverified/inaccessible source** —
  https://businessday.ng/business-economy/article/new-customs-valuation-system-pegs-age-limit-of-cars-at-9-years/
- As of **November 2025**, the 12-year limit reportedly "remains the law," despite rumors of a possible
  reduction to 10 years, and stakeholder groups (Association of Nigerian Licensed Customs Agents/ANLCA,
  vehicle dealers) have lobbied — unsuccessfully as of these reports — for a return to a 15-year limit,
  arguing the 12-year rule "priced millions of Nigerians out of affordable vehicle ownership." — WheelZAR,
  accessed Dec 21, 2025, https://wheelzar.com/nigerias-car-import-policy-age-limits-and-customs-duties/;
  corroborated in framing by The Cable, "Clearing agents to FG: Increase age limit of imported vehicles
  to 15 years," https://www.thecable.ng/clearing-agents-to-fg-increase-age-limit-of-imported-vehicles-to-15-years/
  (exact date not confirmed)
- No outright **ban** on used-vehicle imports was found in force during 2023-2025 — the policy is an age
  ceiling (12 years) plus valuation/duty regime, not a prohibition on used imports generally. (I did not
  find any 2023-2025 announcement of a full tokunbo import ban; a 2016-era PwC projection cited a possible
  "phase-out of used imports ('tokunbo' vehicles) by 2034" as an aspirational long-range plan, not an
  enacted policy — natlawreview.com/Covington, Feb 1, 2016.)

### Used vehicle import volumes/values (see also Section 4 for NBS trade data, which overlaps)
- No separate "unit volume" (number of vehicles) figures were found for 2023-2025 tokunbo imports in this

## 5. AfCFTA linkage for the auto sector

- Nigeria's **National Implementation Strategy for AfCFTA** reportedly sets a goal of increasing vehicle
  production to **200,000 units annually within five years** — WebSearch synthesis, unable to pin to a
  single fully-fetched primary document (ODI PDF fetch returned only binary/unreadable content in this
  pass — see below). **Treat as directionally sourced but not independently confirmed against primary
  text in this pass.**
- Under AfCFTA modalities generally (not Nigeria-specific), **7% of tariff lines** may be designated
  "sensitive," liberalized over 10 years (non-LDCs) or 13 years (LDCs), and **3% of tariff lines** may be
  fully "excluded" from liberalization (capped at 10% of intra-Africa import value). — WebSearch synthesis
  citing tralac.org AfCFTA legal texts resources, https://www.tralac.org/resources/by-region/cfta.html
- **Automobile-sector Rules of Origin (RoO) under AfCFTA remain unresolved as of the most recent reporting
  found.** ODI (Overseas Development Institute) research states that AfCFTA State Parties "have not yet
  agreed on final terms for rules of origin for important categories of goods, such as autos and
  textiles/apparel," and that **Nigerian-assembled vehicles cannot yet qualify for regional export under
  rules of origin**, effectively locking Nigeria's assemblers out of the emerging continental auto market
  despite domestic assembly capacity. — ODI, "Reviving Nigeria's Automotive Industry: opportunities and
